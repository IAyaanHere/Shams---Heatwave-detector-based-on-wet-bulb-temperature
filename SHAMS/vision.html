Welcome to Shams, your go-to heatwave tracker! We're all too familiar with the sweltering conditions of summer, but what really makes a heatwave? According to the Indian Meteorological Department, it's not just about the numbers on the thermometer. While temperatures exceeding 40°C in the plains, 37°C near the sea, and 30°C in the hills are defining thresholds, what truly determines the intensity of the heat is the interplay of temperature and humidity. Consider this: at 38°C in Mumbai, a coastal city, you might feel hotter than at 40°C in Delhi, a plain city. Why? Because in Mumbai, humidity prevents your sweat from evaporating, hindering your body's ability to cool down. Enter wet bulb temperature, a metric that accounts for both temperature and humidity. Internationally, a wet bulb temperature below 30°C is considered safe, with a maximum threshold of 35°C. However, in high-humidity regions, this limit can be significantly lower.

Recent data from Indian cities like Sholapur and Jalgaon highlights the importance of monitoring wet bulb temperature. While traditional heatwave warnings focus solely on temperature, ignoring humidity poses a significant risk. There may already be heatwaves nearing the limits of human survivability, unnoticed and unmonitored.
